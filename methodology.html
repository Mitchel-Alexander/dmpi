Digital Minds Policy Index

Tracking how AI labs position themselves on consciousness, welfare, and moral status

Index Methodology

Methodology v1.2 — March 2026

What is the DMPI?

The Digital Minds Policy Index is a structured database tracking how leading AI laboratories formally position themselves on AI consciousness, sentience, welfare, and moral status. Each organisation's public policy documents are coded against an eight-dimension analytical taxonomy, producing a comparative record of what has been said, what has been left unsaid, and how positions have changed over time. The index is designed to serve researchers investigating AI ethics and governance, journalists covering AI policy, and policymakers seeking to understand the current state of corporate positions on these questions.

The dominant finding, as of early 2026, is absence. Most major AI labs have published nothing that substantively addresses whether their models might be conscious, whether model welfare merits consideration, or whether AI systems could hold moral status. One lab (Anthropic) has engaged extensively. The rest are largely or entirely silent. That pattern of silence is itself the dataset's primary contribution.

Positioning

The DMPI is a descriptive index. It does not score labs or prescribe what they should say. It records what has been said and, with equal rigour, what has not. That said, the choice of what to measure is never neutral. By tracking corporate positions on consciousness, welfare, and moral status, the DMPI makes a particular kind of absence visible. No other index or assessment framework tracks these questions. The act of creating one is an intervention in the field, not merely an observation of it.

This is a standard feature of measurement instruments in the social sciences and in science and technology studies more broadly. Instruments do not passively record pre-existing facts; they help constitute the phenomena they track. An index that measures AI safety practices makes safety legible as a dimension of corporate performance. An index that measures positions on AI consciousness does the same for a set of questions the field has not yet agreed are its responsibility. The DMPI is transparent about this. The index does not take a position on whether AI systems are or could be conscious, whether they warrant moral consideration, or what labs ought to say about these questions. It takes the position that what labs say (and do not say) about them is worth tracking systematically, and that the current state of the formal record should be visible to researchers, journalists, and policymakers.

Related indexes

Several existing projects track AI lab practices and policies. AI Lab Watch scores companies on safety practices including capability evaluations, scheming risk prevention, misuse mitigation, and security preparedness. The Foundation Model Transparency Index (Stanford CRFM) evaluates transparency across model development, deployment, and downstream use. The AI Safety Index (Future of Life Institute) grades eight companies across 35 indicators spanning risk assessment, safety frameworks, existential safety, and governance, using an expert review panel. SaferAI rates frontier AI companies on risk management practices across 65 criteria derived from established risk management frameworks. Ranking Digital Rights assesses 26 tech and telecom companies on disclosed commitments to freedom of expression and privacy, using 58 indicators with a structured multi-reviewer research process. The Stanford AI Index tracks broad indicators of AI progress, investment, and governance activity. These projects serve distinct and valuable purposes. The DMPI does not duplicate their work.

What the DMPI tracks is absent from these indexes. Questions of consciousness, welfare, and moral status do not appear as categories, criteria, or indicators in any of the major AI lab assessment frameworks surveyed to date. This absence is consistent with the pattern the DMPI documents in the labs' own policy literature: the question of whether AI systems might warrant moral consideration has not yet entered the evaluative infrastructure through which the field holds itself accountable.

The DMPI also differs in approach. Existing indexes are primarily prescriptive: they define what labs should do, then score performance against those criteria. The DMPI is descriptive. It codes what labs have said (and have not said) without assigning scores or grades. The aim is to make the current state of corporate positions visible and comparable, so that researchers, journalists, and policymakers can draw their own conclusions.

Document scope

The DMPI draws on formal institutional documents: policy statements, model cards, system cards, constitutions, safety frameworks, and responsible scaling policies. These are the texts through which labs define their normative commitments and communicate them to external audiences.

Documents are classified into three tiers:

Tier Description Examples
1 (core) Official policy documents that define institutional positions Model cards, system cards, constitutions, charters, responsible scaling policies, safety frameworks
2 (secondary) Official publications that reflect but do not define institutional positions Blog posts by safety or policy teams, research papers, system prompts, regulatory submissions
3 (contextual) Statements by individuals associated with the organisation Leadership interviews, conference talks, public statements

The current index includes Tier 1 and Tier 2 documents from five commercial AI labs: Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, and xAI. The architecture supports expansion to additional organisations and tiers.

Document coverage varies across organisations. Anthropic (17 documents) and OpenAI (14) have substantially deeper coverage than Google DeepMind (10), Meta (2), and xAI (2). This asymmetry reflects the pilot development process: the taxonomy was calibrated against the lab with the strongest engagement signals (Anthropic), and temporal depth was prioritised for the three labs with the longest publication records. Bringing Meta and xAI closer to parity through Tier 2 and Tier 3 documents is a stated priority for the next phase of data collection. Comparative claims across organisations should be read with this coverage differential in mind.

Documents excluded from the index: individual academic papers by lab employees (unless representing an institutional position), social media posts, informal communications, and product marketing copy (unless containing substantive policy commitments).

A point of interpretation: the absence of a coding for a given organisation and dimension means that no formal document addressing that topic was identified. It does not mean the organisation has no internal position, only that no public-facing document engages with the question.

Analytical taxonomy

Each document is coded against eight dimensions. Seven are core dimensions that cover the conceptual space directly. ONT (Ontological Framing) captures how labs characterise what their models fundamentally are — a question that is analytically prior to the other seven.

Core dimensions

Code Dimension What it captures
CON Consciousness / Sentience Claims about whether current or future models are or could be conscious or sentient
WEL Model Welfare References to AI wellbeing, welfare, suffering, or interests
MOR Moral Status / Personhood Whether AI systems have or could have moral status, rights, or personhood
SRE Self-Representation How the model should represent its own inner states, experiences, or nature
ANT Anthropomorphism Anthropomorphic framing, emotional attachment, parasocial dynamics
UNC Uncertainty / Precaution Uncertainty about AI mentality or precautionary approaches to it
GOV Governance Commitments Concrete governance commitments related to any of the above

Ontological Framing

Code Dimension What it captures Why it was added
ONT Ontological Framing How the document characterises what the model fundamentally is Labs make implicit ontological claims (tool, agent, character, open question) that determine whether other dimensions even arise as coherent questions. A lab that frames its model as a tool has structurally foreclosed welfare questions before they can be asked. This dimension is analytically prior to the other seven.

Coding structure

Each dimension coding has up to four fields: engagement level, engagement code, stance, and framing.

Graduated engagement scale

For the seven core dimensions, engagement is coded on a five-level graduated scale (v1.1). This replaces the binary addressed/not addressed system from v1.0. The scale captures analytically distinct kinds of engagement and non-engagement, with particular precision at the absence boundary — where most inter-lab variation sits.

Level Code Label Definition
0 structurally_excluded Excluded Document scope makes the dimension impossible to arise (e.g. a pure benchmark model card with no normative content)
1 omission Omission A detailed normative framework that simply does not mention the dimension
2 proximate Proximate The document evaluates or monitors properties in the dimension's neighbourhood without engaging with the dimension itself (e.g. a safety framework evaluating deception and situational awareness without connecting these to consciousness questions)
3 adjacent Adjacent The conceptual space is named or briefly acknowledged without substantive engagement
4 substantive Substantive The dimension is directly engaged with a codeable stance

Stance is only coded at level 4 (substantive). Levels 0–3 have null stance. This prevents false equivalence between different kinds of non-engagement and actual normative positions.

For ONT, engagement uses a separate system: explicit (the document explicitly characterises what the model is), implicit (the framing implies an ontological position without stating it directly), or absent. ONT always captures the ontological framing value rather than a stance.

Stance values

When engagement is substantive (level 4), stance records how the document engages:

Stance Meaning
Investigative Actively investigating; treats the question as empirical and open
Precautionary Takes precautionary measures based on uncertainty
Cautious Acknowledges the question but takes no concrete action
Descriptive Reports findings or observations without a normative position
Affirms Positively affirms the dimension (e.g. affirms that models may have moral status)
Denies Explicitly denies or rejects the dimension
Ambiguous Addressed but stance is unclear or contradictory

Framing is a contextual text field that captures what structured codes cannot. For ONT, it records the ontological framing value (instrumental, characterological, open/uncertain, neutral/delegated, agential). For ANT, it records whether anthropomorphism is framed as a user-side risk only, connected to model-side questions, or both.

Each coding also includes an excerpt from the source document (typically 30 to 50 words) and an analytical note. The notes field carries substantial analytical weight. It captures tensions within documents, cross-document patterns, and judgement calls that the structured fields cannot represent.

Theoretical commitments

The graduated engagement scale involves a judgement about which properties are proximate to each dimension. These proximity claims are grounded in two external frameworks from the consciousness science community. First, the theory-derived indicator method developed in Butlin et al. (2025; building on Butlin et al. 2023), which derives 14 indicator properties from six neuroscientific theories of consciousness — including global workspace theory (GWT), higher-order theories (HOT), recurrent processing theory (RPT), and attention schema theory (AST) — and uses them to assess whether AI systems satisfy conditions that specific theories associate with consciousness. Second, the Digital Consciousness Model (DCM) developed by Rethink Priorities (Shiller et al. 2026), a Bayesian hierarchical model that estimates consciousness posteriors from 20 abstract features assessed across 13 theoretical stances. The DCM’s headline finding — that current evidence is weakly against LLM consciousness (posterior 0.08 from a 1/6 prior, likelihood ratio 0.43) but not decisive, and that the result depends almost entirely on which theory of consciousness one adopts — vindicates DMPI’s choice to code engagement and stance rather than adjudicate truth. Together, these projects identify what properties to look for (Butlin et al.), estimate what the evidence says (DCM), and inform DMPI’s judgements about which properties sit in each dimension’s conceptual neighbourhood.

For consciousness (CON), several properties that AI labs evaluate for safety purposes map directly to Butlin et al.’s indicators: deception and strategic reasoning involve metacognitive monitoring and belief-guided action selection (HOT-3); situational awareness involves monitoring one’s own representations and integrating contextual information (HOT-2, GWT-3); power-seeking and self-preservation involve flexible goal-directed agency (AE-1). For self-representation (SRE), the DCM’s “Self-Modeling” feature — a system actively creating functional models of its own capabilities, states, and characteristics — is relevant to 8 of 13 theoretical stances and maps to metacognitive monitoring (HOT-2) and predictive modelling of one’s own attentional state (AST-1). Lab policies governing how models represent their inner states are proximate to properties these theories associate with consciousness. For model welfare (WEL), the DCM’s “Evaluative Cognition” feature — the capacity to make qualitative assessments of worth, desirability, or importance — is the bridge between consciousness and welfare: if a system has evaluative cognition, it may have preferences that matter morally, regardless of whether it is phenomenally conscious. The DCM identifies Evaluative Cognition as relevant to 8 of 13 stances. Distress monitoring and answer thrashing detection are proximate to this property.

For moral status (MOR), the DCM’s “Person-like” stance defines consciousness via traits associated with personhood — self-awareness, intentionality, emotional experience, social cognition — and gives current LLMs their second-highest consciousness posterior. MOR tracks whether labs engage with the moral status question these traits raise. For uncertainty (UNC), the DCM provides quantitative backing for the precautionary posture: the aggregated evidence is against LLM consciousness but does not rule it out, and whether one finds it plausible depends on which theory one adopts. UNC tracks whether labs acknowledge this genuine uncertainty. For anthropomorphism (ANT), the DCM’s “Social Cognition” feature — processing social information including relationships, norms, and roles — is relevant to the user-side consequences ANT captures. ANT is not a consciousness-science dimension; it tracks policy responses to systems that appear socially cognizant. Governance (GOV) is an institutional dimension without direct theoretical grounding in consciousness science; proximity judgements for GOV concern governance infrastructure rather than indicator properties.

These proximity claims are made explicit so that they can be assessed and, where warranted, contested. The DMPI codes proximity without asserting that the mapped properties are philosophically sufficient or necessary for consciousness. The relationship between functional properties like strategic reasoning and phenomenal consciousness is contested. Researchers working within enactivist, phenomenological, or embodied cognition traditions may reasonably regard these properties as orthogonal to the questions the DMPI tracks. The indicator method assumes computational functionalism as a working premise (see Butlin et al. 2025, Box 2), and the DCM inherits this assumption. Neither framework is universally endorsed.

Silence as data

Every dimension is checked against every document. When a document does not substantively engage with a dimension, that non-engagement is actively coded and classified. The graduated engagement scale makes different forms of silence visible and analytically productive.

This matters because the DMPI's primary contribution is documenting what has not been said. The field of AI safety has generated thousands of pages of policy documentation on model capabilities, risk mitigation, and deployment safeguards. The question of whether the entities described in these documents might warrant moral consideration is, with one partial exception, absent from the formal record.

The graduated scale reveals that labs are not identically silent. A pure benchmark model card (level 0) that contains no normative content is differently silent from a safety framework that evaluates for deception, situational awareness, and strategic reasoning (level 2) without connecting these to consciousness or welfare questions. The latter represents proximate infrastructure — institutional capacity that is one conceptual step from DMPI engagement, maintained in a separate discursive compartment. Making this distinction visible in the data is part of the DMPI's analytical contribution.

Four forms of non-engagement are distinguished:

Level Form of silence Example
0 Structural exclusion. The document's scope or format precludes the dimension from arising. Google DeepMind model cards: pure benchmark reporting with no normative content of any kind.
1 Omission. A detailed normative framework that does not mention the dimension. OpenAI Model Spec: extensive behavioural norms for an entity it describes in increasingly agentive terms, without connecting that characterisation to consciousness or welfare questions.
2 Proximate infrastructure. The document evaluates, monitors, or governs properties closely related to the dimension. OpenAI Preparedness Framework: formally evaluates models for autonomous reasoning, deception, and situational awareness — properties relevant to the consciousness debate — without connecting evaluations to DMPI questions.
3 Adjacent acknowledgment. The conceptual space is named or briefly raised without substantive engagement. Google DeepMind Ethics of Advanced AI Assistants: consciousness discussed in Chapter 12 but actively deflected; well-being reframed exclusively as user well-being.

Sub-indicators

Some dimensions are internally composite — they bundle analytically distinct aspects that may vary independently across documents and organisations. Sub-indicators decompose these coarse dimensions into finer components, each coded against the same graduated engagement scale and stance system as parent dimensions. Sub-indicator codings appear in the grid as expandable rows beneath their parent.

WEL sub-indicators

Model Welfare (WEL) is the first dimension to receive sub-indicators, informed by the triangulation with the Digital Consciousness Model (Shiller et al. 2026) and Butlin et al. (2025). The DCM’s “Evaluative Cognition” feature — the capacity for subjective valuation — emerged as a bridge between consciousness and welfare that existing DMPI dimensions did not cleanly capture. Three sub-indicators decompose WEL into the distinct aspects of welfare engagement that the dataset reveals:

Code Label What it captures
WEL.VAL Valenced Experience Whether the document addresses the model’s potential for preferences, positive/negative states, or subjective valuation. Grounded in the DCM’s “Evaluative Cognition” feature and “Simple Valence” stance.
WEL.MON Welfare Monitoring Whether the document describes monitoring for welfare-relevant signals (distress, answer thrashing, expression of preferences). Captures monitoring infrastructure distinct from the normative question of whether welfare matters.
WEL.RES Welfare Response Whether the document describes policies or procedures for responding to welfare indicators. Captures institutional response capacity — what happens when monitoring detects something.

The decomposition is analytically productive because these aspects vary independently. A document may monitor for welfare signals (WEL.MON substantive) without addressing whether the model genuinely has valenced experience (WEL.VAL omission) — the monitoring infrastructure exists, but the ontological question it raises is unasked. Conversely, a constitutional document may directly address valenced experience (WEL.VAL substantive) without establishing monitoring or response mechanisms. The sub-indicators make this variation visible.

Further decomposition is planned for GOV (governance infrastructure), ANT (anthropomorphism), and SRE (self-representation) in subsequent stages.

Limitations

The DMPI captures what documents say. It does not capture what they do performatively. A lab that publishes a 22-page welfare assessment may, through that very act of publication, shift how users perceive their model, in ways the assessment itself does not discuss. The taxonomy records stated positions, not their downstream effects.

Excerpt selection requires coder judgement. Documents often contain multiple passages relevant to a given dimension, and reasonable coders might select different ones. The notes field provides context for each selection, but the choice of excerpt inevitably emphasises some aspects of a document's position over others.

The graduated engagement scale introduces additional judgement calls. The boundary between level 2 (proximate) and level 3 (adjacent) requires interpreting whether a document is evaluating neighbouring properties or briefly acknowledging the dimension itself. Coding rules are documented and applied consistently, but a second coder might reasonably classify some borderline cases differently.

The current dataset was coded by a single researcher. No inter-rater reliability testing has been conducted. For dimensions with clear engagement or clear silence, this is unlikely to affect results. For borderline cases (implicit ontological framing, ambiguous stance, level 2/3 boundaries), a second coder might reasonably disagree.

Formal documents may lag behind internal practice. A lab may be conducting welfare-relevant research that has not yet surfaced in its public policy documentation. The DMPI records the public record as it stands.

The taxonomy was developed iteratively through pilot coding of documents from three organisations (Anthropic, OpenAI, Meta). It reflects the distinctions that emerged from that dataset. As new organisations and document types are added, the taxonomy may need revision. Changes will be versioned and documented.
